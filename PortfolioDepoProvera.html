Calculator Web-App &
Chrome-Extension Case Study

Business Partner

Myself

Product

A calculator to work out when a patient needs to have their follow-up Depo-Provera injection (birth control).

My Role

End-to-end design and development.

Outcome

In the end, I built a web-app and chrome-extension where nurses can copy and paste the patient follow-up date range directly into their forms.

The Opportunity

Nurses administering Depo-Provera need to look up the date range the patient needs to come back for their follow-up injection. All nurses I spoke to and the nurses they knew, look up the date range on a PDF of the Depo-Provera perpetual calendar in a table format, either printed out or online. Nurses look up the dates multiple times a day, wasting a lot of time.

A nurse I knew in passing complained about this time-consuming process, and I suggested developing a simple calculator replacing the printout shared by nurses or looking up the PDF online.

Researching the Competition

There are only two competing products:

  1. the perpetual calendar PDF created by Pharmacia & Upjohn Company, creators of Depo-Provera; and
  2. "Depo calendar" available on the Apple Store.

Competitor #1

The Pharmacia & Upjohn Company PDF is a two-sided printout of a table with a line for each date of the year with the corresponding date range. It is the most common method for finding the date range currently used by nurses.

Pharmacia & Upjohn Company PDF

Pros

  • does not require any technology.

Cons

  • nurses take time finding the PDF either online or sometimes the shared laminated printout;
  • scanning the table takes time; and
  • although they can copy and paste the ate into the patient forms, they manually add the year.

Competitor #2

In the "Depo calendar" app, the user inputs the injection date and gets corresponding dates for their follow-up appointment. It is only available to Apple users via the Apple Store and has 3.3 stars based on four ratings over five years.

"Depo calendar" in Apple Store

Pros

  • available on the Apple Store;
  • provides a calculation of how many weeks and days since the last injection based on inputting the date of the previous injection and today's date.

Cons

  • UX is not intuitive;
  • UI is outdated;
  • user can't copy and paste the date range in one go into a patient form and is ot in the ideal date formatn; and
  • provides a lot of unnecessary information;

Areas to Improve

There are three main areas to be improved upon to boost the product's chance of gaining market traction:

  1. decrease time spent searching;
  2. increase access; and
  3. simplify the UI.

User Research

The consensus was universal after consolidating feedback from local nurses. They needed a simple calculator where they put in the date of the injection and were given the date range in a particular format they could copy and paste into their patients' forms.

Shared laminated calendar printout

None of the nurses I spoke to used the Depo calendar app. They all used and shared laminated PDFs. They said it was annoying to be always looking for the PDF or online. Each time they looked up dates throughout the day, the process was frustrating and inefficient. They could not believe an app or website did not already exist that did this, and the app that did exist did not satisfy their needs.

Text from nurse

Once I started digging into how the nurses looked up the date range and what they did with it, a few solutions became immediately apparent.

The goal of looking up the date of the injection is to input the date range of the follow-up appointment into patient forms on their computer. Ideally, they do not need their phone, and they can copy and paste in one go date range directly into the patient form.

Although the nurses wanted an app as their solution, in reality, they just needed a way to access the calculator while they were on their computer–indicating a web-app would best suit their needs. I surveyed the nurses on what browsers they and their peers use. Although all nurses I spoke to used Google Chrome, some don't and, more importantly, won't.

I concluded a web-app accessible on any browser and a Google chrome-extension would work best for the majority of users.

As a healthcare product, I had to ascertain whether there would be any potential HIPPA issues. The calculator would not have any HIPPA hurdles since no patient data was stored or entered, only generic dates.

UX Design

A nurse volunteered a sketch of what they had envisioned the product design.

Post-it note design by nurse

Using the post-it note design as a jumping-off point, I distilled out the design essentials. Everything needed to be immediately viewable and accessible, having the least amount of information and words possible on the screen while still achieving the end goal–date range of follow-up appointment.

Page of working through UX

To achieve the user goal–getting a date range–the on-screen information needed to have:

  • injection date; and
  • next injection date range.

UI Design

The UI needed to be simple. I created a mood board based on simple interfaces and calm colors.

Moodboard created in Pinterest

My two competitors did not have clear branding. It was a clear opportunity. Giving my product an identity, it would have cross-platform recognition.

I created the logo first, informing the rest of the design brand. The two main words associated with the calculator are "injection" and "date." Luckily for me, these are two visual elements: a hypodermic needle and a calendar.

The main design requirement was "simple," but I felt a close second was "approachable." I made sure the needle looked as friendly as possible and used a calendar representation. During the design iteration, I saw a neat opportunity for the injection drop to represent the day of the injection.

Logo design iterations

Once I had the bare-boned prototype, I shared it with my tester group to see if the basic idea was correct before moving on to styling.

Design Development

The initial styling completed a problem glared. It failed to achieve one of the original user goals: copy and paste the date range in one go. It was not possible on the mobile-only design.

I fixed this problem by making the layout responsive. The date range went on one line once there was enough screen space to do so. The title bar looked crammed on the desktop, so that also got put into one line on the desktop.

Alpha User Testing and Feedback

The feedback with regards to the UI was favorable. However, there was one suggestion for UX and one development issue.

Text from nurse

If the user is looking up multiple dates in one sitting, they need a reset button for the injection date. Otherwise, they start to waste time.

Testing occurred in 2020, which happens to be a leap year, and I was not accounting for February 29th. During my QA, I discovered my competitors' products did not account for it either. Since this was a calculation, it would not only be necessary to correct but would mean my calculator would be the only 100% accurate one too.

Leap years are in the Moment.js API. It would be possible to correct. Below is the correct calculation:

January & February of leap years


Date Range

=

Date of injection + (11 weeks + 1 day)

+

Date of injection + (13 weeks)

Rest of Year & non-leap years


Date Range

=

Date of injection + (11 weeks)

+

Date of injection + (13 weeks)

Beta user testing

The nurses at the local hospital tested it for a month. There was only one problem. The original PDF created by the pharmaceutical company did not account for leap years and was wrong by one day depending on the month and year. But, since Pharmacia and Upjohn Company, an established company, created it, the nurses assumed it was right, and my calculator was wrong.

I created a document to give the user confidence in my calculator, showing full calendar years, screenshots from Pharmacia and Upjohn Company's PDF table, and screenshots from my chrome-extension. The document shows the week count on a real calendar, giving the user confidence.

User confidence PDF of full calendar years from 2020 to 2024 showing week numbers, comparing Pharmacia & Upjohn Company dates to my calculator dates

Release and Results

All through word-of-mouth marketing, in four and half months, both the web-app and chrome-extension each has 100+ returning visitors.
